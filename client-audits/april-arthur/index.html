Constraint Diagnosis
P1
No BOF Direct-Offer Creative — Zero Booking-Intent Ads
The entire paid creative stack is TOF/MOF: educational reels, empathy reels, and a grocery lead magnet. Not a single ad in the visible Meta Ad Library drives directly to a call booking or applies for a program. This means leads are entering DM automation without any prior intent signal — the setter's job is to cold-convert awareness leads into booked calls, which is why the blended lead-to-book rate sits at 1.5%. The creative is not doing the pre-qualification work it should. At a 10.6:1 CAC:LTV ratio, April can afford to run BOF creative aggressively — the cost to acquire a qualified call is irrelevant if the LTV math holds.
Fix: Build 2–3 BOF direct-offer entities immediately. Hook: pain acknowledgment → mechanism tease → direct CTA ("DM me METHOD to book a free thyroid review call"). Test on existing warm audience (followers + video viewers) first. These ads do not need new production — April can film against a plain background in 15 minutes each. Target: setter lead-to-book rate above 3% within 60 days of launching BOF creative.
P1
Setter Throughput Gap — 1.5% Blended Lead-to-Book Rate
The success coach data shows three setters with wildly divergent CBH rates: Marcelle at 0.16, Elizabeth at 0.39, and Kamii at 0.09. That spread means setter performance is not a system problem — it is a rep-level problem on the low end and a script/targeting problem at the system level. With 4,125 DM funnel leads in Q1 and only 212 scheduled calls, the business is leaving thousands of qualified-lead conversations on the table every month. March's close rate collapse to 22% (from 41.5% in February) is partly explained by setter output quality, not just call volume.
Fix: (1) Script audit for Marcelle and Kamii — compare against Elizabeth's 0.39 CBH approach. What is Elizabeth saying that converts at 2.5× the rate? Clone that script. (2) Add a BOF warm-up email/DM sequence between lead opt-in and setter outreach to increase intent before first touch. (3) Set a floor CBH of 0.25 for any setter as a 30-day performance standard. A setter at 0.09 is destroying the economics of the $0.94 CPL advantage.
P2
Backend Conversion Gap — FE→BE at 44.6% vs. 50% Target
The Q1 average FE→BE conversion of 44.6% sits 5.4 points below the IFCA 50% benchmark, with January at 41% and March collapsing to 36.9%. The average backend deal size of $2,900 is also soft — success coach notes explicitly flag "Big AC Sales Gap" and a $2,900 avg BEO as constraints. At 50 frontend closes in Q1, a 5.4% conversion gap represents approximately 3 missed backend clients per month — roughly $8,700/month in lost contracted backend revenue at current deal sizes.
Fix: (1) The closer 2 is in their second month — implement structured BE pitch training tied to the 4-phase journey. The Restore → Refine → Elevate → Master framing is a natural upsell narrative: "You've just completed Phase 1 — here's what Phase 2 makes possible." (2) Add an AC resell coaching system — proactively identify frontend clients who are hitting their Phase 1 outcomes at weeks 8–10 and pitch the backend before the program ends. (3) Review whether $2,900 avg BE deal is a pricing problem or a conviction problem.
P2
Overhead Margin Running 11–16% vs. 3–8% Target
Q1 overhead margin averaged 12.9% (January 11.8%, February 16.5%, March 11.1%) — significantly above the 3–8% IFCA benchmark. February's 16.5% spike is particularly notable. This may reflect lab costs, software overhead, or fixed costs that are not scaling proportionally with revenue. With Q1 monthly overhead averaging $12,113, the business is carrying overhead that is approximately 2× the target rate at current revenue levels. This directly compresses the net operating margin, which is borderline at Q1 average ~49% vs. the 50%+ target.
Fix: Line-item audit of overhead categories. Identify which costs are fixed vs. variable. At $93.8K/month revenue, 8% overhead = $7,500 — if the business is running $12K+, there is $4,500+/month to recover. February's spike suggests a one-time or irregular expense. Separate lab costs from operational overhead in reporting to understand true cost structure.
P3
March Close Rate Dip — 22.2% on 54 Live Calls
March's 22.2% close rate — versus January's 35.6% and February's 41.5% — is concerning as a directional signal even if the Q1 average holds at 32.5%. Closer 2 entering the rotation in month 2 is likely contributing. The dip is not catastrophic (contracted revenue was $94K in March, not a disaster), but a 22% close rate on 54 live calls means 42 live conversations that did not close. At a $4,400 average deal, those represent $184K in potential contracted revenue left in the room in a single month.
Watch: Monitor April and May close rate closely as Closer 2 develops. If the rate doesn't recover to 30%+ by month 3, run a structured call recording review — compare Closer 2's objection handling against the strongest calls from January/February. The close rate should be above 30% consistently before scaling ad spend above $5K/month total.
OK
CPL & Unit Economics — Leave Alone
The DM funnel CPL of $0.94 (Q1 blended) and follower CPF of $0.43 are elite. The 10.6:1 CAC:LTV ratio is healthy. The contracted ROAS is 65x+ on average. Marketing margin of 3.5–4.2% is actually below the IFCA floor of 10% — this is not a problem, it means the business is dramatically underinvesting in ad spend relative to its economic capacity. At current LTV and CAC, the business could triple ad spend and still operate comfortably inside the IFCA benchmarks.
Direction: Do not optimize for CPL — it is already exceptional. Instead, use the budget headroom to fund the BOF creative buildout and setter infrastructure. The opportunity cost of not spending more is far larger than any marginal CPL improvement would deliver.
