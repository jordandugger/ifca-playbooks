Constraints ranked by revenue impact. P1 = critical bottleneck, P2 = meaningful gap, P3 = watch item, OK = leave alone and protect.

P1
Under-Investment in Advertising — 4.3% of Revenue vs. 15–20% Growth Target
Camden is running a 12× collected ROAS machine and spending 4.3% of revenue on ads. A growth-phase business should be at 15–20%. That gap represents $7,500–$11,000 in additional monthly spend that would generate an estimated 50–80 additional leads and 5–10 additional closes per month — at existing economics. Every month this gap exists, Camden is leaving approximately $9K–$18K in net profit on the table from the additional closes he should be acquiring.
Fix: Set a client capacity target (e.g. 35–40 new clients/month vs. current ~21). Reverse-engineer the required ad spend from Q1 blended CPA (~$143). At 35 closes/month, that's ~$5,000/month minimum ad spend (vs. current ~$3,000). At 40 closes, ~$5,700. Budget to capacity, not to comfort level. Then launch 6+ new entities to prevent the CPL increase that will come with higher spend on stale creative.
P1
Creative Fatigue — CPL Tripled From $11 → $18 With Only 4 Active Entities
Running 4 creative entities against Andromeda's 8–10 target is the direct cause of the CPL deterioration. From January to March, CPL rose 57% and cost per scheduled call rose 115% (from $35.71 to $76.92). The algorithm exhausts a small creative pool faster than a diverse one — and Camden's monthly ad spend is not high enough to justify that burn rate. Without new entities entering the rotation weekly, scaling spend will only accelerate the fatigue.
Fix: Launch the 138K-view organic reel as a paid TOF entity immediately — zero new recording required. Then build to 8–10 active entities over 30 days using the hooks from the organic library. Use the "3 hooks × 8 ad types + location/image variation" system from the success coach Loom to generate 24 new testable variations from existing content. Kill anything above $12 CPL at 72-hour intervals. Minimum $30/day test budget ($150/week for 5 concurrent tests).
P2
Lead → Booked Call Rate at 2.9% — Target is 4–5% on Mixed Funnel
The setting tracker shows 1,444 conversations started in March producing 43 booked calls — a 2.9% lead-to-book ratio on a mixed DM ad + organic + follower funnel. On a purely organic funnel this might be acceptable, but on a mixed funnel where paid DM leads are in the pipeline, 4–5% is the standard. The gap between 2.9% and 4.5% on 1,444 conversations is roughly 23 additional calls/month — at a 57% close rate, that's 13 additional closes, or approximately $24K in additional contracted frontend revenue every month.
Fix: Daily QC audit of 2–3 random setter conversations. Measure setter call booking per hour (target: 0.25 met, 0.35 exceed, 0.45 excels). At 43 calls in ~160 setter hours, current rate is ~0.27 — MET KPI but will not scale without adding a second setter or AI automation. Once call volume grows with higher ad spend, setter capacity becomes the chokepoint. Begin planning this transition now, not after the bottleneck hits.
P3
Show Rate at 62% Q1 — Below 70% Target, March Barely Reaching Target
Q1 show rate averaged 62% (target 70%) with significant monthly variance: 63.5% → 52.6% → 70.8%. The 52.6% February dip is the most concerning — that's 14 calls that showed vs. 27 scheduled. At a 57% close rate, those 13 no-shows represent approximately 7 closes and ~$13K in contracted revenue in a single month. February's dip may correlate with lower-quality lead sourcing or a drop in confirmation/reminder sequences during that period.
Fix: Audit the confirmation and reminder sequence cadence. Is there a 24-hour SMS reminder? A 1-hour reminder? A calendar link in the initial confirmation? Every missed show-up is a $1,839 FE minimum that didn't happen. At scale (40+ scheduled calls/month), a 10-point show rate improvement is worth 4+ closes. Build the reminder sequence once, automate it, and protect the Q1 March 70.8% number as the new floor.
P3
FE → BE Data Gap — Backend Conversion Only Tracked in January
Backend FE→BE conversion is populated (45%) for January but missing for February and March in the tracker. Since backend revenue materially impacts blended LTV and the rev equilibrium calculation, running with a partial picture on the most important LTV driver is a data risk. Backend revenue in Q1 was $74.6K total — a significant portion of the $210K Q1 cash — and without clean month-by-month tracking, it's impossible to know if the attach rate is declining, stable, or growing.
Fix: Populate February and March backend conversion data in the tracker. Add the weekly upsell drilling and game tape sessions that the success coach recommended — this is both a tracking fix and a conversion protection move. At 45% attach and $3,159 avg BE price, every 1% improvement in attach rate across 21 monthly closes = $660/month in additional backend contracted revenue.
OK
Close Rate, CPA, and Operating Margins — Leave Alone and Scale Into
57% close rate trending upward (65% in March), $168 CPA at 9.2% of FE price, 57% net operating margin, 83% upfront cash collection rate. These are all either at or significantly above IFCA targets. No optimization needed here — the risk is over-engineering a system that already works. The correct move is to scale into these strengths (more volume, not different process) and protect them as entity diversity and setter capacity expand.
Protect by: Not changing the close script while volume is low. Not pivoting offer pricing during the ad scale phase. Maintaining the current labor overhead levels while revenue grows — don't add team before demand justifies it. The margins will compress naturally as spend increases; the goal is to keep net margin above 45% as the 15–20% marketing margin target is reached.
